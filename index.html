About

Hello! I am currently a senior (final year) undergraduate student majoring in data science at the University of Illinois Chicago. I am passionate about addressing complexities and challenges in sustainability and conservation through developing machine learning applications. In my time as an undergraduate student, I have had the incredible opportunity of researching the viability of using social media image data for large-scale wildlife population monitoring under the guidance of Dr. Tanya Berger-Wolf. In doing so, I've been able to merge my passion for protecting our endangered species with my fascination of unpacking and applying algorithms for data cleaning, feature extraction, data visualization, and machine learning.

My research has been my primary focus during my undergrad years, and has ultimately culminated into a profound sense of responsibility to take action by harnessing the power of data and using machine learning to surface patterns, ideas, and insights to protect our natural world. As my undergraduate studies come to an end, I plan on pursuing a PhD in computer science that would ultimately grant me the opportunity for not just lifelong learning, but further contribution to the research community with a special focus in sustainability and conservation applications - domains that are only beginning to see some of the benefits from advances in high speed computing and machine learning methods.

As the role of technology transforms and transcends its role in our society, I believe a baseline technical literacy should be provided to any person that is affected by our ever present technologies. As a Latina woman in a field that suffers from underrepresentation, I dedicate time to contribute to my community and offer mentorship to students entering the tech field. Participating in outreach and mentorship programs that work towards increasing equitability in the field of tech is something that I hold with great importance as I continue to share my journey into the field of ML/AI.

Crowdsourcing Social Media Image Data for Generating Wildlife Population Estimates

Among several strategies that seek to address conservational challenges, WildMe’s Wildbook uses machine learning and computer vision to identify individual animals in images and compute population estimates. These estimates are used to fill in gaps on data-deficient species, empowering data-driven insights and accelerating policy to protect wildlife.

With the recent increase in widespread use of social media, we can access thousands of images and their metadata as a source for wildlife population estimates.

In our work, we use public access APIs and Python to collect images and videos from social media platforms including YouTube, Flickr, and iNaturalist. We include search terms and resulting image data for six species: Humpback Whales, Whale Sharks, Iberian Lynx, Reticulated Giraffe, Grevy's Zebra, and Plains Zebra. Our work focuses on building a dataset of images and videos crowdsourced from these platforms, and computing analytics to uncover any biases in our data. With image data sourced from widespread, global platforms, it is important to understand the origin of our data, and who is contributing to the datasets of the species at hand.

Projects

Senior Undergraduate Thesis: Content-based Recommendation System for Crowdsourcing Humpback Whale Videos

Ongoing: Fall 2021 -Spring 2022

I am currently working under the guidance of my undergraduate thesis supervisor, Dr. Gonzalo Bello-Lander, to fine-tune a deep learning model for humpback whale detection in videos. This deep learning model is the first step towards implementing a content-based video recommendation system for faster access to a pool of relevant and related wildlife videos for further population estimate computations.

Machine Learning Pneumonia Classifier using Keras and VGG16

Code on GitHub

During the summer of 2021, I fine-tuned a convolutional neural network in Python using the pretrained Keras VGG16 architecture to predict whether chest X-rays are indicative of pneumonia. My dataset was sourced from a collection of chest xray images by the NIH. Throughout the process, I was able to learn about fundamental deep learning concepts such as the different types of layers in a deep learning model (convolutional layers, max pooling, and dense layers), as well as dropout and how to choose an appropriate activation function.

Teaching & STEM Outreach

Google Research Explore CSR

I have had the opportunity to assist with Explore CSR workshops on two occasions. Explore CSR is an initiative held by Google Research to provide resources and introduce students, especially those from underrepresented communities, to paths in computer science research (CSR).

Explore CSR: Exploring Data Science Research University of Illinois Chicago
Teaching Assistant - November 2021

As a teaching assistant for the exploreCSR program held at UIC, I helped participants complete a set of coding exercises computing summary statistics and data visuals on a dataset consisting of health and socioeconomic data of residents in Chicago.

The workshop consisted of a series of speakers sharing their research and their paths to computer science research in the first half of the program. The students were then broken up into ten teams and tasked with building a decision tree model to predict a class label assigned to each team from our Chicago data.

Besides technical knowledge and explaining machine learning concepts to students attending the workshop, I was also able to share my personal experience in data science so far. As most students had only a couple introductory CS courses under their belt, I shared my experience and advice regarding computer science courses, navigating internships, and pursuing research opportunities.

Explore CSR + Ohio State University: Using Machine Learning to Identify Wildlife in Columbus, OH
Teaching Assistant - November 2020

As a teaching assistant for this workshop, I helped Dr. Berger-Wolf introduce students to applications of machine learning in conservation domains. I was responsible for creating the notebook with demos and exercises for students to connect to the Microsoft Species Classifier API and classify wildlife native to Columbus, OH from images.

We Can Code - Addison Public Library

Instructor

With the help of the Addison Public Library, I coordinated and instructed a six-week workshop introducing students grades 3-6 with no previous coding experience to the fundamentals of Python. The workshop was centered around creating custom pixel designs using the Imagilabs web app.

In between coding exercises during the workshops, I would encourage students who finished early to experiment with the concepts and code to make the designs their own, which I think was a main driver in keeping the kids engaged and curious over what seemed to work and what didn't. Allowing for this "experimenting time" enabled students to challenge their own preconceived notions as to how they thought the code worked, and come up with more questions along the way to cement their understanding.

UIC & Noble High School Data Science: Summer of a Lifetime

Teaching Assistant

During this one-week summer intensive program, I helped out with introducing high school students to the fundamentals of data science. The workshop taught students how to code in Python, as well as how to download and clean datasets for data science applications. We also covered topics such as single and multiple linear regression models, hypothesis testing, and decision trees for classification. At the end of the workshop, student groups presented projects on using decision trees for classification and what they took away from the workshop.
